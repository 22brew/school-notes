c) Write out the model in equation form. Is the relationship between the predictor and the response positive or negative? What does the coefficient for the horsepower variable suggest?

  • predicted mpg (mpg_hat) = 39.9359 - 0.1578 x horsepower

  • The coefficient for horsepower is -0.1578.

  • For every 1-unit increase in horsepower, the vehicle's fuel efficiency is associated with a decrease by approximately 0.1578 mpg on average.

  • since p < 0.000 , yes there is a significant association between horsepower and mpg

d) Comment on the quality of fit of the model.

Interpretation Only (No Coding Required) The R2 value is 0.606. Interpretation: It tells us that 60.6% of the variability in fuel efficiency (mpg) is explained by horsepower. While this indicates a clear relationship, nearly 40% of the variation remains unexplained (the "Residuals"). An R2 of 0.60 suggests that 0.60 of the variability in fuel efficiency (mpg) is explained by a linear regression on horsepower

e) Is there a statistically significant association between the predictor and the response?

  • since p < 0.000 ,less than the typical significance level of 0.05 (or even 0.01), we reject the null hypothesis. This provides strong evidence that there is a statistically significant relationship between horsepower and mpg at 5% level of significance

g) In some cases, the true relationship between the response and the predictors may be nonlinear. One simple way to extend the linear model is to accommodate a transformed predictor. The data seem to have a quadratic pattern, suggesting a model of the form

To implement this in statsmodels.formula.api, apply a transformation to the predictor and wrap within the I()function (e.g. I(predictor^2)) and treat it like a normal predictor variable. Generate a new model (model2) and interpret whether including the squared term improves the fit.

model2 = smf.ols('mpg ~ horsepower + I(horsepower**2)', data=auto_data).fit()
print(model2.summary())

# The fit appears to be substantially better than the fit obtained when just the linear term is included. The adjusted R2 of the quadratic fit is 0.686, compared to
