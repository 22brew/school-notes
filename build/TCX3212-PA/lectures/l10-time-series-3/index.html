L10: Time Series III

Regression-Based Time Series Forecasting​

In general, a time series model for YtY_tYt​ can include:

  • Lagged values of YYY (autoregressive terms): Yt−1,Yt−2,…Y_{t-1}, Y_{t-2}, \ldotsYt−1​,Yt−2​,…
  • Current and lagged values of other variables XXX: regressors indexed by time.

Yt=b0+b1Yt−1+b2Xt+⋯Y_t = b_0 + b_1 Y_{t-1} + b_2 X_t + \cdotsYt​=b0​+b1​Yt−1​+b2​Xt​+⋯

This lecture focuses on regression-based approaches where external predictors — possibly offset in time — help explain YtY_tYt​.

Static Models​

A static model models a contemporaneous (same time period) relationship between YYY and one or more XXX variables:

yt=β0+β1x1t+β2x2t+⋯+βkxkt+εt,t=1,2,…,ny_t = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{1t} + \beta_2 x_{2t} + \cdots + \beta_k x_{kt} + \varepsilon_t, \qquad t = 1, 2, \ldots, nyt​=β0​+β1​x1t​+β2​x2t​+⋯+βk​xkt​+εt​,t=1,2,…,n

"Contemporaneous" means both yyy and all xxx variables are measured at the same time period ttt.

Key interpretation: A change in xktx_{kt}xkt​ at time ttt has an immediate effect on yty_tyt​:

Δyt=βkΔxkt(when Δεt=0)\Delta y_t = \beta_k \Delta x_{kt} \qquad \text{(when } \Delta \varepsilon_t = 0\text{)}Δyt​=βk​Δxkt​(when Δεt​=0)

OLS Assumptions for Time Series​

For OLS to be valid in a time series setting:

AssumptionMeaning
TS1: Mean-Zero ErrorE[εt∣Xt]=0\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_t \mid X_t] = 0E[εt​∣Xt​]=0
TS2: HomoskedasticityVar(εt∣Xt)=σ2\text{Var}(\varepsilon_t \mid X_t) = \sigma^2Var(εt​∣Xt​)=σ2 (constant)
TS3: Uncorrelated ErrorsCov(εt,εs)=0\text{Cov}(\varepsilon_t, \varepsilon_s) = 0Cov(εt​,εs​)=0 for t≠st \neq st=s
TS4: Weak Dependence{(Xt,Yt)}\{(X_t, Y_t)\}{(Xt​,Yt​)} is stationary and weakly dependent
TS5: LinearityModel is correctly specified as linear

When TS1–TS5 hold, OLS estimators work the same as in cross-sectional data (unbiased, efficient, standard inference valid).

Example 1: Phillips Curve (Inflation vs Unemployment)​

inft=β0+β1unempt+εt\text{inf}_t = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \text{unemp}_t + \varepsilon_tinft​=β0​+β1​unempt​+εt​

Study the contemporaneous relationship between US annual inflation rate and unemployment rate (1948–2003). This is a classic static model in macroeconomics — the Phillips Curve hypothesis.

Example 2: Interest Rates​

i3^t=1.73+0.606⋅inft+0.513⋅deft\hat{i3}_t = 1.73 + 0.606 \cdot \text{inf}_t + 0.513 \cdot \text{def}_ti3^t​=1.73+0.606⋅inft​+0.513⋅deft​

where i3i3i3 = 3-month T-bill rate, inf\text{inf}inf = annual CPI inflation rate, def\text{def}def = government deficit as % of GDP.

Interpretation:

  • Intercept (1.73): If both inflation and deficit are zero, the baseline T-bill rate is ~1.73%.
  • Inflation coefficient (0.606): A 1 percentage-point increase in inflation is associated with a 0.606 point increase in the T-bill rate, holding the deficit constant. (p-value < 0.05 → significant.)
  • Deficit coefficient (0.513): A 1 percentage-point increase in the deficit/GDP ratio is associated with a 0.513 point increase in the T-bill rate. (p-value < 0.05 → significant.)

Example 3: Fertility Rate with Dummy Variables​

gfr^t=98.68+0.083⋅pet−24.24⋅ww2t−31.59⋅pillt\widehat{\text{gfr}}_t = 98.68 + 0.083 \cdot \text{pe}_t - 24.24 \cdot \text{ww2}_t - 31.59 \cdot \text{pill}_tgfr​t​=98.68+0.083⋅pet​−24.24⋅ww2t​−31.59⋅pillt​

where:

  • gfr\text{gfr}gfr = general fertility rate (children per 1,000 women)
  • pe\text{pe}pe = personal tax exemption ($)
  • ww2\text{ww2}ww2 = 1 during World War II (1941–1945), 0 otherwise
  • pill\text{pill}pill = 1 after 1963 (availability of oral contraceptive pill), 0 otherwise

Interpretation:

  • ww2 (−24.24): Holding other variables constant, fertility was ~24 fewer births per 1,000 women during WWII compared to peacetime.
  • pill (−31.59): After 1963, fertility was ~31.6 fewer births per 1,000 women per year, controlling for tax exemptions and wartime status. The pill dummy is highly significant (p < 0.05).

Time Series with Trend​

The Problem: Spurious Regression​

When two variables both have upward trends, regressing one on the other can produce a spuriously significant coefficient — even if the variables have no genuine causal relationship. Including a time trend variable ttt in the regression controls for this.

Linear Time Trend Model​

yt=β0+β1t+εty_t = \beta_0 + \beta_1 t + \varepsilon_tyt​=β0​+β1​t+εt​

  • E(yt)=β0+β1tE(y_t) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 tE(yt​)=β0​+β1​t is a linear function of time.
  • β1>0\beta_1 > 0β1​>0: on average, yty_tyt​ has an upward trend of β1\beta_1β1​ per period.
  • β1<0\beta_1 < 0β1​<0: downward trend.
  • First difference: yt−yt−1=β1y_t - y_{t-1} = \beta_1yt​−yt−1​=β1​ (constant), so linear trend → constant increment.

Including Trend as a Covariate​

Add ttt as a predictor when yty_tyt​ and one of the xxx variables share a common trend:

yt=β0+β1x1t+β2x2t+β3t+εty_t = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{1t} + \beta_2 x_{2t} + \beta_3 t + \varepsilon_tyt​=β0​+β1​x1t​+β2​x2t​+β3​t+εt​

Omitting the trend when it's present causes omitted variable bias — the xxx coefficients absorb the trend, making them biased and misleading.

Example: Housing Investment and Prices​

Without trend: log⁡(invpc)^t=−0.550+1.241log⁡(price)t\widehat{\log(\text{invpc})}_t = -0.550 + 1.241 \log(\text{price})_tlog(invpc)​t​=−0.550+1.241log(price)t​ Appears to show a strong positive relationship — but both variables trend upward over time. This is a spurious relationship driven by the common trend.

With trend: log⁡(invpc)^t=−0.913−0.381log⁡(price)t+0.0098t\widehat{\log(\text{invpc})}_t = -0.913 - 0.381 \log(\text{price})_t + 0.0098 tlog(invpc)​t​=−0.913−0.381log(price)t​+0.0098t

  • The price elasticity is now negative and not statistically significant.
  • The trend is significant: per-capita housing investment grows by ~0.98% (~1%) per year, independent of price.
  • Conclusion: there is no evidence that price drives housing investment — the apparent relationship was entirely due to both variables trending upward together.

Time Series with Seasonality​

When data exhibits periodic seasonal patterns (monthly, quarterly), include seasonal dummy variables.

For monthly data with January as the base month, add 11 dummies (February through December):

yt=β0+β1x1t+⋯+δ1Febt+δ2Mart+⋯+δ11Dect+εty_t = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{1t} + \cdots + \delta_1 \text{Feb}_t + \delta_2 \text{Mar}_t + \cdots + \delta_{11} \text{Dec}_t + \varepsilon_tyt​=β0​+β1​x1t​+⋯+δ1​Febt​+δ2​Mart​+⋯+δ11​Dect​+εt​

  • β0\beta_0β0​ = intercept for January (base month).
  • δk\delta_kδk​ = the average difference in yyy during month kkk compared to January, holding other variables constant.

General rule: For LLL seasons (months/quarters), create L−1L - 1L−1 seasonal dummies. The omitted season is the reference.

For quarterly data: yt=β0+β1xt+δ1Q2t+δ2Q3t+δ3Q4t+εty_t = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_t + \delta_1 Q2_t + \delta_2 Q3_t + \delta_3 Q4_t + \varepsilon_tyt​=β0​+β1​xt​+δ1​Q2t​+δ2​Q3t​+δ3​Q4t​+εt​

Seasonal dummies and the time trend can be combined to capture both components simultaneously.

Leading and Lagging Variables​

Sometimes xtx_txt​ and yty_tyt​ are related but offset in time:

  • Leading variable: Changes in xxx precede changes in yyy. (xxx leads yyy.)
  • Lagging variable: Changes in xxx follow changes in yyy. (xxx lags yyy.)
  • Coincident: Both move together in the same period.

How to identify: Draw a vertical line near a prominent feature on a time series plot. Whichever variable "reaches" the landmark first is the leading variable.

Examples:

Leading VariableLagging VariableLag
Haze levelMask salesA few days
Forest fires in IndonesiaHaze in SingaporeA few days
Cigarette tax increaseCigarette consumptionA few months
Study hoursExam gradeDays

Finite Distributed Lag (FDL) Models​

When xxx is a leading variable for yyy, we can model the delayed effects of xxx on yyy explicitly:

yt=β0+β1xt+β2xt−1+β3xt−2+εty_t = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_t + \beta_2 x_{t-1} + \beta_3 x_{t-2} + \varepsilon_tyt​=β0​+β1​xt​+β2​xt−1​+β3​xt−2​+εt​

Coefficient Interpretation​

CoefficientNameInterpretation
β1\beta_1β1​Impact effectImmediate effect of a 1-unit change in xtx_txt​ on yty_tyt​, holding all past xxx constant.
β2\beta_2β2​1-period dynamic multiplierEffect of xt−1x_{t-1}xt−1​ on yty_tyt​ — the response one period after the shock.
β3\beta_3β3​2-period dynamic multiplierEffect of xt−2x_{t-2}xt−2​ on yty_tyt​ — the response two periods after the shock.
β1+β2+β3\beta_1 + \beta_2 + \beta_3β1​+β2​+β3​Long-run (cumulative) multiplierTotal effect on yyy of a permanent 1-unit increase in xxx, after all lags have worked through.

Example: Haze and Mask Sales​

Suppose people respond to haze with a 1-day lag (they need time to go buy masks):

MaskSalest=b0+b1⋅Hazet−1+b2⋅Mother+b3⋅Age+εt\text{MaskSales}_t = b_0 + b_1 \cdot \text{Haze}_{t-1} + b_2 \cdot \text{Mother} + b_3 \cdot \text{Age} + \varepsilon_tMaskSalest​=b0​+b1​⋅Hazet−1​+b2​⋅Mother+b3​⋅Age+εt​

Additional control variables (Mother, Age) are not time-indexed — they are treated as cross-sectional covariates and interpreted the same as in standard multiple regression.

Autoregressive Model​

A special case of lagged regression using lagged values of YYY itself:

Salest=b0+b1⋅Salest−1+εt\text{Sales}_t = b_0 + b_1 \cdot \text{Sales}_{t-1} + \varepsilon_tSalest​=b0​+b1​⋅Salest−1​+εt​

This is called an AR(1) (autoregressive of order 1) model. Key difference from SMA:

  • SMA takes an unweighted average of the last www observations.
  • AR(1) estimates a regression coefficient b1b_1b1​ that is learned from the data — not necessarily 1/w1/w1/w.
