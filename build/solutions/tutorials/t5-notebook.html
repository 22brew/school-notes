c) Which category (Male or Female) is serving as the reference (baseline) group? How can you tell? Based on the coefficient for sex[T.Male], do men or women have more positive attitudes toward immigration?

Female is serving as the reference (baseline) group.

We can tell because the model formula explicitly specifies Treatment(reference='Female'), and in the regression output the dummy variable shown is C(sex, Treatment(reference='Female'))[T.Male] — only Male appears as a coefficient, meaning Female is the omitted/baseline category.

The coefficient for sex[T.Male] is +0.2148, which is positive and statistically significant (p < 0.05). This means that, holding age constant, men score approximately 0.21 points higher on the better scale than women. Therefore, men have more positive attitudes toward immigration than women.

i) In words, explain: How does the effect of age on better change across political groups?

The baseline group (Any other party) has a slope of -0.0204, meaning age has a relatively small negative effect on immigration attitudes for this group.

For Labour voters, the slope is -0.0516 (= -0.0204 + (-0.0312)), which is the steepest negative slope. This means older Labour voters are considerably less positive about immigration compared to younger Labour voters — age has the strongest negative effect in this group.

For Conservative voters, the slope is -0.0314 (= -0.0204 + (-0.0110)), which falls between the other two groups. Age has a moderate negative effect on immigration attitudes for Conservatives.

In summary, while age has a negative effect on immigration attitudes across all political groups, this effect is strongest among Labour voters and weakest among those who voted for any other party. This suggests that political affiliation moderates the relationship between age and attitudes toward immigration.
