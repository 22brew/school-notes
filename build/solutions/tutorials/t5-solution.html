b) Comment: Based on the coefficient sex[T.Male], the baseline for the "sex" variable is Female.

c) Which category (Male or Female) is serving as the reference (baseline) group? How can you tell? Based on the coefficient for sex[T.Male], do men or women have more positive attitudes toward immigration?

Solution:

  • Categorical variables (like Gender) cannot be calculated as numbers directly. To include them, the model creates a dummy variable.
  • By default, statsmodels looks at your categories—Female and Male—and picks the one that comes first alphabetically as the Reference Group (also called the baseline)
  • Since "Female" comes before "Male", Female beecomes the reference.
  • The regression model then creates a variable called sex[T.Male].
  • The [T.Male] notation stands for "Treatment: Male." The "T" stands for "Treatment" group. In dummy coding, the category that is not listed in the table is the baseline.
  • Since "Male" is listed as the comparison, "Female" must be the reference group hidden within the Intercept.

  • The variable educ has three possible values: Lower secondary, Upper secondary, Tertiary (Higher education/University)
  • The regression model will need (k-1)= 2 dummy variables.

Explanation¶

  • One category (usually "Lower secondary" because it is first alphabetically) will be chosen as the reference group. The regression output will then show two coefficients:
  • educ[T.Upper secondary]: The difference between Upper secondary and Lower secondary.
  • educ[T.Tertiary]: The difference between Tertiary and Lower secondary. By leaving one out, the model avoids the "Dummy Variable Trap" (perfect multicollinearity), where the variables would give redundant information.

Interaction Term¶

An interaction term in regression is used when the effect of one variable depends on the value of another variable. We want to see whether the effect of age on attitudes towards immigration (better) differs for people with different political preferences (vote).

i) In words, explain: How does the effect of age on 'better' change across political groups?

The interaction model tells us that the effect of age is not the same for everyone; it depends significantly on which party a person supports.¶
Any other party¶
  • The effect of age on better is b3= -0.0204 for persons supporting any other party, for every one year increase in age, it decreases by 0.0204 points in the perceived impact of immigration (better)
Labour¶
  • The effect of age on better is (b3+b5) = -0.0516 for persons supporting labour, For every one-year increase in age, it decreases by 0.0516 points in the perceived mpact of immigration (better)
Conservative¶
  • The effect of age on better is (b3+b4)= -0.0314 for persons supporting conservative. For every one-year increase in age, it decreases by 0.0314 points in the perceived mpact of immigration (better)
  • While this slope is steeper than the baseline, the interaction coefficient has a p-value of 0.107, meaning this difference in slopes is not statistically significant at the 5% level.
