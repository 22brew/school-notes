## Questions

In this exercise, you will examine time series models that are useful in empirical series analysis and easily estimated by ordinary least squares.

**1.** You will use the **fertil3** (`feril3.csv`) dataset — an annual time series of the United States fertility rate from 1913–1984. The goal is to model the effects of personal tax exemption, war, and medical innovation on fertility rates.

| Variable | Description |
|---|---|
| gfr | General fertility rate — children born per 1,000 women of childbearing age |
| pe | Personal exemption — average real dollar value of the individual tax exemption |
| ww2 | Dummy variable (1 for years 1941–1945 WWII; 0 otherwise) |
| pill | Dummy variable (1 from 1963 onward — availability of the birth control pill; 0 otherwise) |

**(a)** A **static model** is used when a change in an independent variable $x_t$ is believed to have an immediate (contemporaneous) effect on the dependent variable $y_t$. Load `feril3.csv` and set a yearly frequency index starting in 1913.

**(b)** Fit the following static model where $gfr_t$ is the dependent variable:

$$\widehat{gfr}_t = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 pe_t + \hat{\beta}_2 ww2_t + \hat{\beta}_3 pill_t$$

**(c)** Based on your estimated coefficient $pe$, show the calculation to prove that a \$12.00 increase in the tax exemption leads to approximately one additional birth per 1,000 women.

**(d)** Is the variable $ww2_t$ statistically significant at the 1% level? Interpret the coefficient $ww2_t$ — what was the estimated contemporaneous effect of World War II on the general fertility rate, holding the personal exemption constant?

**(e)** Interpret the coefficient for $pill_t$. What was the estimated contemporaneous effect on the fertility rate after 1963, holding other factors constant?

---

**2. Finite Distributed Lag (FDL) Model**

In a Finite Distributed Lag (FDL) model, we allow one or more variables to affect $y$ with a lag (over time rather than just immediately). The fertility rate may react to changes in the personal tax exemption with a delay.

**(f)** To account for these delayed effects, create a 1-year lag $pe_{t-1}$ and a 2-year lag $pe_{t-2}$ for the personal exemption variable using Python.

**(g)** Fit the following distributed lag model of order two:

$$\widehat{gfr}_t = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 pe_t + \hat{\beta}_2 pe_{t-1} + \hat{\beta}_3 pe_{t-2} + \hat{\beta}_4 ww2_t + \hat{\beta}_5 pill_t$$

**(h)** Examine the p-values for $pe_t$, $pe_{t-1}$, and $pe_{t-2}$. Are they individually significant at the 5% level?

**(i)** Run the correlation matrix for the personal exemption variables. How does this substantial correlation explain the p-values you found in part (h)?
