Learning Objectives​

  • Understand Regression Analysis with Time Series Data
  • Apply and Interpret Regression Models for Time Series Analysis

Questions​

In this exercise, you will examine time series models that are useful in empirical series analysis and easily estimated by ordinary least squares.

1. You will use the fertil3 (feril3.csv) dataset — an annual time series of the United States fertility rate from 1913–1984. The goal is to model the effects of personal tax exemption, war, and medical innovation on fertility rates.

VariableDescription
gfrGeneral fertility rate — children born per 1,000 women of childbearing age
pePersonal exemption — average real dollar value of the individual tax exemption
ww2Dummy variable (1 for years 1941–1945 WWII; 0 otherwise)
pillDummy variable (1 from 1963 onward — availability of the birth control pill; 0 otherwise)

(a) A static model is used when a change in an independent variable xtx_txt​ is believed to have an immediate (contemporaneous) effect on the dependent variable yty_tyt​. Load feril3.csv and set a yearly frequency index starting in 1913.

(b) Fit the following static model where gfrtgfr_tgfrt​ is the dependent variable:

gfr^t=β^0+β^1pet+β^2ww2t+β^3pillt\widehat{gfr}_t = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 pe_t + \hat{\beta}_2 ww2_t + \hat{\beta}_3 pill_tgfr​t​=β^​0​+β^​1​pet​+β^​2​ww2t​+β^​3​pillt​

(c) Based on your estimated coefficient pepepe, show the calculation to prove that a $12.00 increase in the tax exemption leads to approximately one additional birth per 1,000 women.

(d) Is the variable ww2tww2_tww2t​ statistically significant at the 1% level? Interpret the coefficient ww2tww2_tww2t​ — what was the estimated contemporaneous effect of World War II on the general fertility rate, holding the personal exemption constant?

(e) Interpret the coefficient for pilltpill_tpillt​. What was the estimated contemporaneous effect on the fertility rate after 1963, holding other factors constant?

2. Finite Distributed Lag (FDL) Model

In a Finite Distributed Lag (FDL) model, we allow one or more variables to affect yyy with a lag (over time rather than just immediately). The fertility rate may react to changes in the personal tax exemption with a delay.

(f) To account for these delayed effects, create a 1-year lag pet−1pe_{t-1}pet−1​ and a 2-year lag pet−2pe_{t-2}pet−2​ for the personal exemption variable using Python.

(g) Fit the following distributed lag model of order two:

gfr^t=β^0+β^1pet+β^2pet−1+β^3pet−2+β^4ww2t+β^5pillt\widehat{gfr}_t = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 pe_t + \hat{\beta}_2 pe_{t-1} + \hat{\beta}_3 pe_{t-2} + \hat{\beta}_4 ww2_t + \hat{\beta}_5 pill_tgfr​t​=β^​0​+β^​1​pet​+β^​2​pet−1​+β^​3​pet−2​+β^​4​ww2t​+β^​5​pillt​

(h) Examine the p-values for petpe_tpet​, pet−1pe_{t-1}pet−1​, and pet−2pe_{t-2}pet−2​. Are they individually significant at the 5% level?

(i) Run the correlation matrix for the personal exemption variables. How does this substantial correlation explain the p-values you found in part (h)?
