L6: Regression II

Statistical Inference in Regression​

After fitting a regression model, two key inferential questions arise:

  1. Overall model significance (F-test): Is at least one predictor X1,…,XpX_1, \ldots, X_pX1​,…,Xp​ useful in predicting YYY?
  2. Individual predictor significance (t-tests): Is there a statistically significant association between each specific predictor XjX_jXj​ and YYY?

Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Intervals for Coefficients​

Standard Errors of Coefficient Estimates​

The OLS estimate β^1\hat{\beta}_1β^​1​ is an unbiased estimate of the true population slope β1\beta_1β1​. However, estimates vary across different samples. The standard error quantifies this variability:

SE(β^1)=Var(β^1)\text{SE}(\hat{\beta}_1) = \sqrt{\text{Var}(\hat{\beta}_1)}SE(β^​1​)=Var(β^​1​)​

For the Advertising example:

  • β^0=7.0326\hat{\beta}_0 = 7.0326β^​0​=7.0326, SE(β^0)=0.458\text{SE}(\hat{\beta}_0) = 0.458SE(β^​0​)=0.458
  • β^1=0.0475\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.0475β^​1​=0.0475, SE(β^1)=0.003\text{SE}(\hat{\beta}_1) = 0.003SE(β^​1​)=0.003

β^1\hat{\beta}_1β^​1​ will be closer to the true β1\beta_1β1​ when (1) the sample size nnn is larger, or (2) the predictor values xix_ixi​ are more spread out.

Confidence Intervals for Coefficients​

The 100(1−α)%100(1-\alpha)\%100(1−α)% confidence interval for βj\beta_jβj​ is:

β^j±t(n−p−1, α/2)⋅SE(β^j)\hat{\beta}_j \pm t_{(n-p-1,\, \alpha/2)} \cdot \text{SE}(\hat{\beta}_j)β^​j​±t(n−p−1,α/2)​⋅SE(β^​j​)

where t(n−p−1, α/2)t_{(n-p-1,\, \alpha/2)}t(n−p−1,α/2)​ is the critical t-value with n−p−1n - p - 1n−p−1 degrees of freedom.

Interpretation (95% CI): With repeated sampling, 95% of such constructed intervals will contain the true unknown βj\beta_jβj​.

For the Advertising data:

  • 95% CI for β0\beta_0β0​: [6.130,7.935][6.130, 7.935][6.130,7.935] → we are 95% confident that when TV spending is $0, average sales are between 6,130 and 7,935 units.
  • 95% CI for β1\beta_1β1​ (TV): [0.042,0.053][0.042, 0.053][0.042,0.053] → we are 95% confident that each additional $1,000 in TV spending is associated with 42–53 additional units sold.

Since 0 is not in the CI for β1\beta_1β1​, TV advertising is statistically significant.

t-Test for Individual Predictors​

Tests whether a single predictor XjX_jXj​ is associated with YYY:

H0:βj=0H1:βj≠0H_0: \beta_j = 0 \qquad H_1: \beta_j \neq 0H0​:βj​=0H1​:βj​=0

t-statistic:

t=β^jSE(β^j)t = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j}{\text{SE}(\hat{\beta}_j)}t=SE(β^​j​)β^​j​​

Decision rule: If p-value <α=0.05< \alpha = 0.05<α=0.05, reject H0H_0H0​ → predictor is statistically significant at 5%.

For TV advertising: p-value ≈0.000<0.05\approx 0.000 < 0.05≈0.000<0.05 → reject H0H_0H0​ → statistically significant relationship.

Note: A p-value is the probability of observing a t-statistic at least as extreme as the one calculated, assuming H0H_0H0​ is true. It is not the probability that H0H_0H0​ is true.

F-Test on Multiple Regression Coefficients​

When there are ppp predictors, use the F-test to evaluate overall model significance:

H0:β1=β2=⋯=βk=0H1:at least one βj≠0H_0: \beta_1 = \beta_2 = \cdots = \beta_k = 0 \qquad H_1: \text{at least one } \beta_j \neq 0H0​:β1​=β2​=⋯=βk​=0H1​:at least one βj​=0

The F-statistic compares the model's explained variance to its unexplained variance:

F=(TSS−RSS)/pRSS/(n−p−1)F = \frac{(\text{TSS} - \text{RSS})/p}{\text{RSS}/(n-p-1)}F=RSS/(n−p−1)(TSS−RSS)/p​

  • Under H0H_0H0​, F≈1F \approx 1F≈1.
  • A large FFF (and small p-value) → reject H0H_0H0​ → at least one predictor is useful.

Example: For the Advertising dataset with TV, Radio, Newspaper: F-test p-value =1.58×10−96<0.05= 1.58 \times 10^{-96} < 0.05=1.58×10−96<0.05 → strong evidence that at least one medium is associated with sales.

Why not just check individual t-tests? With many predictors, even if all βj=0\beta_j = 0βj​=0, some t-tests will appear significant by chance (multiple testing problem). The F-test controls for this.

Coding Scheme for Categorical Variables​

Regression requires numerical inputs. Qualitative (categorical) predictors must be encoded as dummy (indicator) variables.

Two-Level Categorical Variable (e.g., Gender)​

Create one binary indicator:

xi={1if female0if male (reference)x_i = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if female} \\ 0 & \text{if male (reference)} \end{cases}xi​={10​if femaleif male (reference)​

Model: Balance=β0+β1⋅Income+β2⋅xfemale+ε\text{Balance} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{Income} + \beta_2 \cdot x_{\text{female}} + \varepsilonBalance=β0​+β1​⋅Income+β2​⋅xfemale​+ε

Interpretation:

  • β^0=233.77\hat{\beta}_0 = 233.77β^​0​=233.77: Predicted average balance for a male with income = $0.
  • β^1=6.05\hat{\beta}_1 = 6.05β^​1​=6.05: For each one-unit increase in income, average balance increases by $6.05, holding Gender constant.
  • β^2=24.31\hat{\beta}_2 = 24.31β^​2​=24.31: Females carry on average $24.31 more in credit card balance than males with the same income level.

The choice of reference level (male = 0 vs female = 0) does not affect model fit — only the interpretation of the intercept and dummy coefficient.

import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# C() tells Python to treat Gender as categorical; Treatment sets the reference level
model = smf.ols('Balance ~ Income + C(Gender, Treatment(reference="Male"))',
                data=credit).fit()

Three-or-More Level Categorical Variable (e.g., Ethnicity)​

For kkk categories, create k−1k - 1k−1 dummy variables. The omitted category is the reference group.

Example: Ethnicity ∈ {African American, Asian, Caucasian}

PersonAsianCaucasianEthnicity
A00African American (reference)
B10Asian
C01Caucasian

Interpretation:

  • β^1\hat{\beta}_1β^​1​ = average difference in balance: Asian vs African American
  • β^2\hat{\beta}_2β^​2​ = average difference in balance: Caucasian vs African American
  • β^2−β^1\hat{\beta}_2 - \hat{\beta}_1β^​2​−β^​1​ = average difference in balance: Caucasian vs Asian

To test if Caucasian and African American differ: H0:β2=0H_0: \beta_2 = 0H0​:β2​=0.

Interaction Terms in Regression​

Additive model (no interaction): assumes the effect of one predictor is the same regardless of another predictor's value.

Balance=β0+β1⋅Income+β2⋅Student+ε\text{Balance} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{Income} + \beta_2 \cdot \text{Student} + \varepsilonBalance=β0​+β1​⋅Income+β2​⋅Student+ε

Both students and non-students share the same slope β1\beta_1β1​ for Income. They only differ in their intercepts.

Interaction model: allows the effect of Income on Balance to depend on student status.

Balance≈β0+β1⋅Income+β2⋅Student+β3⋅(Income×Student)+ε\text{Balance} \approx \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{Income} + \beta_2 \cdot \text{Student} + \beta_3 \cdot (\text{Income} \times \text{Student}) + \varepsilonBalance≈β0​+β1​⋅Income+β2​⋅Student+β3​⋅(Income×Student)+ε

Expanding: Balance={(β0+β2)+(β1+β3)⋅Incomeif Studentβ0+β1⋅Incomeif Not Student\text{Balance} = \begin{cases} (\beta_0 + \beta_2) + (\beta_1 + \beta_3) \cdot \text{Income} & \text{if Student} \\ \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{Income} & \text{if Not Student} \end{cases}Balance={(β0​+β2​)+(β1​+β3​)⋅Incomeβ0​+β1​⋅Income​if Studentif Not Student​

  • Effect of Income on Balance for students: β1+β3\beta_1 + \beta_3β1​+β3​
  • Effect of Income on Balance for non-students: β1\beta_1β1​
  • β3\beta_3β3​: how much the income slope differs for students vs non-students.

Marginal effect rule: For any non-linear or interaction model, the marginal effect of XkX_kXk​ on YYY is the partial derivative ∂Y∂Xk\frac{\partial Y}{\partial X_k}∂Xk​∂Y​.

# Interaction term in statsmodels
smf.ols('Balance ~ Income * C(Student, Treatment(reference="No"))',
        data=df).fit()

Interpretation of interaction: If β^3=−1.99\hat{\beta}_3 = -1.99β^​3​=−1.99 and β^1=6.22\hat{\beta}_1 = 6.22β^​1​=6.22, the income slope for students is 6.22+(−1.99)=4.236.22 + (-1.99) = 4.236.22+(−1.99)=4.23 — students see a smaller boost in balance from each additional unit of income compared to non-students. (Even if the interaction term is not statistically significant, it is the correct way to compute the student slope.)

Practice Questions​

Q1. The 95% confidence interval for the TV coefficient is [0.042,0.053][0.042, 0.053][0.042,0.053] (TV measured in $1,000s). Which statement is correct?

  • A) ✓ We are 95% confident that every additional $1,000 in TV advertising is associated with a 42 to 53 unit increase in sales on average.
  • B) There is a 95% probability that a $1,000 increase in TV advertising will increase sales by 42–53 units.
  • C) Because the interval excludes zero, TV advertising is not statistically significant.

Why A: A confidence interval is a statement about the parameter (over repeated samples), not a probability about any single observation. And excluding zero means TV advertising is significant, not the opposite.

Q2. In the model Balance ~ Income + Gender with Male as reference, β^0=233.77\hat{\beta}_0 = 233.77β^​0​=233.77. What does this represent?

  • A) The predicted average balance for a Female with income = $0.
  • B) ✓ The predicted average balance for a Male with income = $0.
  • C) The average difference in balance between females and males.
  • D) Females carry $233.77 more credit debt than males.

Why B: β^0\hat{\beta}_0β^​0​ is the intercept, interpreted at the reference level of all categorical variables (Male) and the baseline of all quantitative variables (Income = 0).

Q3. To test whether at least one of the advertising media is useful for predicting sales, which hypotheses are correct for an F-test?

  • A) ✓ H0:βTV=βRadio=βNewspaper=0H_0: \beta_{\text{TV}} = \beta_{\text{Radio}} = \beta_{\text{Newspaper}} = 0H0​:βTV​=βRadio​=βNewspaper​=0; H1:H_1:H1​: at least one βj≠0\beta_j \neq 0βj​=0.
  • B) H0:βTV=0H_0: \beta_{\text{TV}} = 0H0​:βTV​=0; H1:βTV≠0H_1: \beta_{\text{TV}} \neq 0H1​:βTV​=0. (This is a t-test, not F.)
  • C) H0:βintercept=βTV=βRadio=βNewspaper=0H_0: \beta_{\text{intercept}} = \beta_{\text{TV}} = \beta_{\text{Radio}} = \beta_{\text{Newspaper}} = 0H0​:βintercept​=βTV​=βRadio​=βNewspaper​=0; H1:H_1:H1​: more than one βj≠0\beta_j \neq 0βj​=0.

Why A: The F-test tests whether all slope coefficients are simultaneously zero. The intercept is not included in the null hypothesis.

Q4. In the interaction model Balance ~ Income + Student + Income*Student, what is the marginal effect of income on balance for a student?

  • A) b1=6.22b_1 = 6.22b1​=6.22 (the interaction term is not significant).
  • B) ✓ b1+b3=6.22+(−1.99)=4.23b_1 + b_3 = 6.22 + (-1.99) = 4.23b1​+b3​=6.22+(−1.99)=4.23 (income slope plus the student interaction).
  • C) b3=−1.99b_3 = -1.99b3​=−1.99 (only the interaction term).

Why B: For students (Student = 1), the model becomes Balance = (β0+β2)+(β1+β3)⋅Income(\beta_0 + \beta_2) + (\beta_1 + \beta_3) \cdot \text{Income}(β0​+β2​)+(β1​+β3​)⋅Income. The slope of Income for students is β1+β3\beta_1 + \beta_3β1​+β3​, regardless of statistical significance of β3\beta_3β3​.
