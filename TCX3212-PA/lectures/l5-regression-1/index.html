L5: Regression I

Introduction​

Regression is a supervised learning technique where the response variable YYY is quantitative (continuous). The goal is to estimate the function fff such that Y≈f(X)Y \approx f(X)Y≈f(X), enabling both prediction and inference.

Simple Linear Regression​

Simple linear regression assumes a single predictor XXX and a linear relationship with response YYY:

Y≈β0+β1XY \approx \beta_0 + \beta_1 XY≈β0​+β1​X

  • β0\beta_0β0​ — intercept: the expected value of YYY when X=0X = 0X=0.
  • β1\beta_1β1​ — slope: the average change in YYY for a one-unit increase in XXX.

Both β0\beta_0β0​ and β1\beta_1β1​ are unknown population parameters estimated from data.

Motivating Example: Advertising​

Dataset: Advertising.csv — 200 markets with TV ad spend (thousands $) and Sales (thousands units).

Hypothesis: Sales≈β0+β1⋅TV\text{Sales} \approx \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{TV}Sales≈β0​+β1​⋅TV

Estimating Coefficients: Least Squares​

We want to find β^0\hat{\beta}_0β^​0​ and β^1\hat{\beta}_1β^​1​ such that the fitted line y^i=β^0+β^1xi\hat{y}_i = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 x_iy^​i​=β^​0​+β^​1​xi​ is "as close as possible" to the observed data.

Residuals: ei=yi−y^ie_i = y_i - \hat{y}_iei​=yi​−y^​i​

Residual Sum of Squares (RSS): RSS=e12+e22+⋯+en2=∑i=1n(yi−y^i)2\text{RSS} = e_1^2 + e_2^2 + \cdots + e_n^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2RSS=e12​+e22​+⋯+en2​=∑i=1n​(yi​−y^​i​)2

The Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) estimators minimise RSS:

β^1=∑i=1n(xi−xˉ)(yi−yˉ)∑i=1n(xi−xˉ)2,β^0=yˉ−β^1xˉ\hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})^2}, \qquad \hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x}β^​1​=∑i=1n​(xi​−xˉ)2∑i=1n​(xi​−xˉ)(yi​−yˉ​)​,β^​0​=yˉ​−β^​1​xˉ

Interpreting Coefficients​

For the Advertising example:

Sales^=7.033+0.04754⋅TV\widehat{\text{Sales}} = 7.033 + 0.04754 \cdot \text{TV}Sales=7.033+0.04754⋅TV

  • Intercept (7.033): When TV spending = $0, average predicted sales = 7,033 units. (May have no practical meaning.)
  • Slope (0.04754): Each additional $1,000 in TV spending is associated with ~47.5 additional units sold on average.

General interpretation:

  • β^0\hat{\beta}_0β^​0​ — predicted mean of YYY when all XXXs = 0.
  • β^k\hat{\beta}_kβ^​k​ — a one-unit increase in XkX_kXk​ is associated with ∣β^k∣|\hat{\beta}_k|∣β^​k​∣ unit increase (if positive) or decrease (if negative) in YYY, holding all other variables constant.

Measuring Model Performance​

R2R^2R2 (Coefficient of Determination)​

R2=1−RSSTSS,TSS=∑i=1n(yi−yˉ)2R^2 = 1 - \frac{\text{RSS}}{\text{TSS}}, \qquad \text{TSS} = \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \bar{y})^2R2=1−TSSRSS​,TSS=∑i=1n​(yi​−yˉ​)2

  • R2∈[0,1]R^2 \in [0, 1]R2∈[0,1]: proportion of variance in YYY explained by XXX.
  • R2=0R^2 = 0R2=0: the model explains none of the variability (no better than the mean).
  • R2=1R^2 = 1R2=1: the model perfectly explains the variability.

⚠️ R2R^2R2 always increases as more predictors are added — even irrelevant ones. Use Adjusted R2R^2R2 for model comparison.

Adjusted R2R^2R2 penalises for adding unnecessary predictors:

Rˉ2=1−(1−R2)(n−1)n−p−1\bar{R}^2 = 1 - \frac{(1 - R^2)(n - 1)}{n - p - 1}Rˉ2=1−n−p−1(1−R2)(n−1)​

where ppp is the number of predictors. Adjusted R2≤R2R^2 \leq R^2R2≤R2 always.

Mean Squared Error (MSE)​

MSE=1n∑i=1n(yi−y^i)2\text{MSE} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2MSE=n1​∑i=1n​(yi​−y^​i​)2

Measures the average squared prediction error. More directly interpretable than R2R^2R2 when prediction accuracy matters.

F-Statistic​

Tests whether any predictor is useful in the model (relevant in multiple regression):

  • H0H_0H0​: β1=β2=⋯=βk=0\beta_1 = \beta_2 = \cdots = \beta_k = 0β1​=β2​=⋯=βk​=0 (no predictor is useful)
  • H1H_1H1​: at least one βj≠0\beta_j \neq 0βj​=0

A large F-statistic (and small p-value < 0.05) means we reject H0H_0H0​ — the model as a whole is significant.

Multiple Linear Regression​

In practice, we have multiple predictors. Instead of running separate simple regressions (which ignores relationships among predictors), we use multiple linear regression:

Y=β0+β1X1+β2X2+⋯+βpXp+εY = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + \beta_2 X_2 + \cdots + \beta_p X_p + \varepsilonY=β0​+β1​X1​+β2​X2​+⋯+βp​Xp​+ε

For the Advertising dataset:

Sales=β0+β1⋅TV+β2⋅Radio+β3⋅Newspaper+ε\text{Sales} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{TV} + \beta_2 \cdot \text{Radio} + \beta_3 \cdot \text{Newspaper} + \varepsilonSales=β0​+β1​⋅TV+β2​⋅Radio+β3​⋅Newspaper+ε

Coefficients are again estimated by minimising RSS.

Why Not Run Separate Simple Regressions?​

  • Cannot make a single prediction of YYY that accounts for all predictors simultaneously.
  • Each isolated regression ignores correlations among predictors, leading to unexpected and often misleading results.

Example: Conflicting Results for Newspaper​

When Newspaper is regressed alone on Sales, it appears significant. But in the full multiple regression model, Newspaper is not significant.

Why? Because Radio and Newspaper spending are positively correlated (markets that spend more on radio tend to spend more on newspaper too). Newspaper is acting as a surrogate for Radio — it gets statistical "credit" that really belongs to Radio.

# Correlation matrix reveals this
correlation_matrix = advertising_data.corr()

This illustrates confounding — a predictor may appear important in isolation but be insignificant once other correlated predictors are included.

R² in Multiple Regression​

Adding more predictors always increases R2R^2R2, even if they are useless:

PredictorsR2R^2R2
TV only0.612
TV + Radio0.897
TV + Radio + Newspaper0.897 (tiny increase)

The near-zero improvement from adding Newspaper confirms it is not contributing useful information beyond what Radio already captures. For model selection, use Adjusted R2R^2R2, AIC, or BIC.

Assumptions of Linear Regression​

Linear regression relies on four assumptions for valid inference (p-values and confidence intervals):

AssumptionDescription
LinearityThe true relationship between YYY and XXX is linear in the parameters.
Normal errorsThe error terms εi\varepsilon_iεi​ are normally distributed.
HomoscedasticityThe variance of errors is constant across all values of XXX (constant variance).
IndependenceErrors are independent of each other.

When these hold, OLS is the Best Linear Unbiased Estimator (BLUE) — the Gauss-Markov theorem.

Problems with Linear Regression​

The following issues can violate assumptions or degrade model performance:

Non-Linearity​

The true relationship is curved. Diagnostic: residual plot (residuals vs fitted values) shows a systematic pattern rather than random scatter around zero. Fix: add polynomial terms or transform predictors.

Correlated Errors​

Residuals are correlated over time/space — common in time series data. Diagnostic: Durbin-Watson test; residuals vs time plot shows alternating positive/negative clusters.

Non-Constant Variance (Heteroscedasticity)​

Variance of residuals increases (or decreases) with y^\hat{y}y^​. Diagnostic: residual plot fans out. Fix: log-transform YYY or use weighted least squares.

Outliers​

Points where YYY is far from the predicted value. Outliers inflate RSS and distort coefficient estimates. Diagnostic: standardised residuals beyond ±3.

High-Leverage Points​

Points where XXX is far from the usual range of XXX. These can have a disproportionate influence on the fitted line even if their YYY is well-predicted. Diagnostic: leverage statistic (hat values).

Collinearity​

Two or more predictors are highly correlated with each other. This makes it difficult to determine which predictor is truly driving the response.

Effects of collinearity:

  • Standard errors of coefficients inflate → confidence intervals widen → harder to detect significance.
  • Coefficient estimates become unstable (sensitive to small changes in the data).

Diagnostic: Variance Inflation Factor (VIF). VIF>10\text{VIF} > 10VIF>10 signals serious collinearity.

Fix: Remove one of the correlated predictors, combine them (e.g., average), or use ridge regression (covered in L6).

Python: Fitting Linear Regression​

import statsmodels.formula.api as smf
import pandas as pd

# Load data
advertising = pd.read_csv('Advertising.csv')

# Simple linear regression: Sales ~ TV
model = smf.ols('Sales ~ TV', data=advertising).fit()
print(model.summary())

# Multiple linear regression: Sales ~ TV + Radio + Newspaper
model_multi = smf.ols('Sales ~ TV + Radio + Newspaper', data=advertising).fit()
print(model_multi.summary())

Key outputs from model.summary():

  • coef: estimated coefficients β^\hat{\beta}β^​
  • std err: standard errors of coefficients
  • t / P>|t|: t-statistic and p-value for each coefficient
  • [0.025, 0.975]: 95% confidence interval for each coefficient
  • R-squared / Adj. R-squared: model fit
  • F-statistic / Prob (F-statistic): overall model significance

Practice Questions​

Q1. In the regression Sales ~ TV + Radio + Newspaper, the multiple regression shows no significant relationship between Newspaper and Sales, while a simple regression of Sales ~ Newspaper does show a significant relationship. Does this make sense?

Answer: Yes — this is a classic confounding / omitted variable problem. Newspaper spending is correlated with Radio spending (markets that spend more on radio also tend to spend more on newspaper). In the simple regression, Newspaper gets credit for the effect of Radio. Once Radio is controlled for in the multiple regression, the Newspaper effect disappears. The multiple regression result is more trustworthy.

Q2. What does the fitted equation Sales^=7.033+0.04754⋅TV\widehat{\text{Sales}} = 7.033 + 0.04754 \cdot \text{TV}Sales=7.033+0.04754⋅TV tell us?

Answer: For every additional $1,000 spent on TV advertising, average Sales increase by approximately 47.5 units (0.04754 × 1000). The intercept 7,033 is the predicted Sales when TV spending is zero — may not be practically meaningful.
